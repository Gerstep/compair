Best Practices

To understand the potential usefulness of the ComPAIR learning application, researchers and teaching teams at the University of British Columbia sought input from the students, teaching assistants, and instructors in courses that piloted the intitial tool at UBC.

Feedback received during pilot phase from: 168 students // 6 teaching assistants // 3 instructors

Students, teaching assistants, and instructors participated in surveys, focus groups, and/or one-on-one interviews and these helped reveal the practices that led to a more positive learning experience.

Pilot Findings

  • Students who better understood what skill they were practicing had a more positive experience. To increase student buy-in when using ComPAIR, it is key for students to realize the end goal of using the tool ("how will this help me in this course?") and not only how to use it.
  • Assignments felt more beneficial to students when presented as part of a larger process. It may be more useful for students to use ComPAIR to see what their peers have done and reflect on their own work in the context of a bigger goal, rather than apply this to standalone assignments.
  • Students given more guidance (multiple criteria, additional rubrics) said they learned more from comparing. Helping students understand what to look for during the comparisons can result in better learning when assessing the answer pairs.
  • Regardless of generally low reported confidence in giving peer feedback, many students said they learned simply from practicing this skill with answer pairs. A minimum of three comparisons per ComPAIR assignment is suggested to give students sufficient chances to practice.
